Sovereign AI in India · 2026

Sovereign AI Thesis for India

India already runs Aadhaar identity, UPI payments and benefit transfers on its own digital systems, with Aadhaar alone having issued over 1.44 billion IDs (IBEF / PIB, 9 Mar 2026). No country builds all of AI by itself, so this is a shared national effort: own how AI is built, deployed and governed inside India for the services citizens use, while still drawing on frontier models wherever the rules allow.

  • India's edge is in getting AI to people, not in outspending anyone on money or chips. That edge comes from owning the whole stack: AI developed, deployed and governed in India, built Indian-language-first, and pointed at the jobs enterprises, governments and developers actually need done (Sarvam — "AI for all from India"). Frontier models still get used wherever they do the job better and the rules allow it.
  • The moat competitors can't replicate is deployment and governance inside India. Across government and much of regulated industry the rules already mandate it: RBI's committee called for an Indian finance model (Dvara Research — RBI FREE-AI, 13 Aug 2025), and that builds on the 2018 requirement that payments data stay in India (RBI, 6 Apr 2018) and the new personal-data rules (DPDP Rules, 14 Nov 2025). An India-built model is one governed layer within that stack: useful to control, but not the whole game.
  • Each delivery compounds into the next. A working system shipped on-site for one regulated customer becomes both the proof that wins the next customer and a system that is sharper at those specific tasks the next time around.
Money is in US$; rupees convert at US$1 ≈ ₹84, the 2025 average (site convention; IndiaAI Mission ₹10,371.92 cr ≈ US$1.23B).

01 · Executive summary

What to do, and why

  • Sovereign AI is a national effort shared across many companies, and no one builds the whole thing alone.
  • India does not have the capital to win on chips, but it can still own the full-stack India position: AI that is built, run and governed in India, Indian-language-first, and sold to enterprises, governments and developers, with the chips rented openly.
  • That choice sets the rest of this section, which works through which sectors to enter, what to build, how to win the work, and which kind of Indian company fits.

The decision

The decision, then how it is sequenced and won

The call
  • The company positioned to win here is an Indian one that builds, runs and governs its AI in India, works Indian-language-first, and owns the delivery, distribution and controls layer. The India-governed model is one layer of that stack and an advantage worth having, since its own data makes it better over time.
  • A government mandate puts that position out of reach for a frozen foreign API or a thin fine-tune of someone else's model.
Sequenced
Move 1

Start with the open sectors

  • Government & Public DPI and Fintech & BFSI, where the State and the RBI committee have set rules that require an Indian model, so the rule is a floor that helps you rather than a barrier that blocks you.
  • The State owns the regulator, the licence and the largest firms, but most of the operating base is private (private banks hold about 40% of assets) and many already run frontier models, with Bajaj Finance on Azure OpenAI and Razorpay on Claude (Microsoft India; Fintechnews).
→
Move 2

Land one customer, then resell that exact setup

  • Deploy on site for one regulated firm, then sell the same setup to other regulated firms that have not built their own AI yet.
→
Move 3

Move into conditional sectors

  • Health, Telecom, Energy and Defence, entering each one where its own rules require an India-resident model, then selling across the operating base, which is mostly private (telecom is about 92% private), behind the State-owned network (APAC News, on PIB data).
Why this works
  • It wins the same way the earlier government-anchor example did, because the rule itself creates the customer, and running the model in India while meeting the controls rules is what gets you through procurement.
  • Engineers work on site and put the best model the rules allow inside the customer's systems, which makes the deployment hard to replace; you win on delivery and governance, and that on-site delivery is the work that still has to be done.

The sectors to play

Enter a sector only where the law bars a foreign model, not where you could simply beat one on merit. Two sectors are open now, and the rest are conditional, with a single way in for each.

Government & Public DPI

open now

A foreign model cannot legally run on citizen data

The opening
  • No Indian-language sovereign system runs the citizen-service and benefit systems yet.
  • The State owns all of it. Aadhaar has 1.44 billion IDs. UPI ran 21.70 billion transactions worth US$308.45B in January 2026. DBT moved US$534.49B.
  • The State buys AI for citizen data under the DPDP Act and the IndiaAI Mission's open-weight rule, and a foreign model on government citizen data is blocked on security grounds rather than weighed on price (IBEF, citing PIB 9 Mar 2026).

Fintech & BFSI

open now

The regulator's own committee asked for a homegrown model

The opening
  • Of the five sovereign sectors this is the clearest case, because the regulator itself asked for an Indian model, so it creates the demand rather than leaving the choice to price.
  • The RBI FREE-AI Committee Report (13 Aug 2025), Recommendation 4 calls for an indigenous, finance-specific AI model and a way to fund it. It cites data sovereignty and "excessive global LLM reliance".
  • This sits on top of RBI's binding 2018 rule that payments data must stay in India.
  • About 79% of regulated firms are not yet using or building AI (KPMG digest of the RBI report; structure corroborated by Dvara Research).

Health · Telecom · Energy · Defence · Transport

conditional · one opening each

Narrower entries, taken only where each rule set requires one

The opening per sector
  • Health — health data is the most sensitive data class under the DPDP Act 2023. ABDM is government-owned (≈797M ABHA accounts, ≈650M linked records), so the model must run in India (IndiaMedToday — MoHFW Lok Sabha, 28 Jul 2025).
  • Telecom & Energy — both are legally Critical Information Infrastructure under IT Act S.70A (NCIIPC), so a foreign cloud model is barred from call data or grid data (TRAI Yearly Performance Indicators 2024-25; Down To Earth — Grid India).
  • Defence — classified and air-gapped. 75% of capital acquisition is reserved for Indian industry (Inc42 — India's defence-AI reality check).
  • Transport & Logistics — Critical Information Infrastructure under IT Act S.70A (NCIIPC's critical-sector list names Transport). Ports, aviation and the State-owned ULIP network bar a foreign-cloud model. The logistics-cost pool is about US$286B (ORF — NCIIPC critical sectors, IT Act S.70A; Outlook Business — DPIIT-NCAER, logistics 7.97% of GDP).

The stack to build

The model layer is crowded and the models are close to a commodity, while the chips come from one supplier where money alone will not win. So build the layers an outsider cannot copy.

An India-governed model — one layer

improves with use

An India-governed model keeps learning from the regulated tasks it runs

The build
  • A model governed in India gets better on regulated tasks as you use it, whereas a deployer who only calls a fixed foreign API never gets that compounding gain.
  • Where the model is built this way, it is a from-scratch Indic family: from-scratch Apache-2.0 Sarvam-1 (2B) and from-scratch sparse-MoE Sarvam-30B (~32B) / Sarvam-105B (~106B).
  • The first-funded sovereign-LLM builder states clearly that Sarvam-M (24B) is a Mistral post-train, not built from scratch (Sarvam — model family).

Own the data & the delivery

durable

Indic data and the route into government and regulated deployments

The build
  • The durable advantage comes from language and regulation rather than the algorithms: 22 languages, voice-first users with low literacy, and a model that runs in India under the DPDP Act, all of which shut out English-first frontier APIs.
  • The build is the route from a model into a regulated, multilingual, government-bought deployment, which means on-prem serving, the Indic data and evaluation base, and the integration work inside a ministry or a bank.
  • India can own this layer, but it will not build itself, because the economics do not work until a mandate forces the deployment.

The rented compute layer

rented from NVIDIA

The one layer no Indian player can buy

The build
  • No Indian player owns chips; the compute supply chain ends at NVIDIA.
  • The first-funded sovereign-LLM builder runs on subsidised IndiaAI compute: 4,096 NVIDIA H100 for six months, ≈US$11.7M against a ≈US$29.4M project, the largest IndiaAI subsidy so far (TICE — IndiaAI compute subsidy).
"If the chips are rented, how is it sovereign?"
  • Sovereignty is not in the chips but in the layers built on top of them, which are built, run and governed in India.
  • The model runs in India under the DPDP Act, and the deployment sits inside India's binding security rules: CERT-In, NCIIPC, MeitY empanelment, and the 2018 payments-data rule.
  • Anyone can rent compute, but the regulated-sector deployment, data and governance layer cannot be rented.
  • Krutrim is the warning case: it ended up reselling cloud as a thin wrapper. Sovereignty is in the layers built on top of the rented chips, not in the chips themselves.

How to win the work

Sovereign AI is not sold off a price list; the rules open the door, and on-site delivery wins the work through it. The full approach is set out in the go-to-market section.

How to win the work

explained in the GTM section

A proven template, now used by frontier AI labs too

The conclusion
  • The approach is a proven template: win a government customer first, deliver on site inside the customer's systems, turn that into one repeatable platform, then sell commercially. It compounds. Each mandated deployment earns regulated-sector data, which trains a stronger sovereign model, which wins the next deal.
  • Palantir proves it works: FY2025 $4.475B revenue, 54% government, 46% commercial, 954 customers, net dollar retention 139% (Palantir FY2025 10-K SEC 403 · quoted from official IR PDF; forward-deployed-engineer origin per The Pragmatic Engineer).
  • Frontier AI labs now use the same approach: Claude into US defence at Impact Level 6 (TechCrunch, 7 Nov 2024), a DoD agreement with a $200M ceiling (Anthropic, 12 Aug 2025), and Anthropic and OpenAI enterprise-services ventures on the forward-deployed-engineer model "popularized by Palantir" (TechCrunch, 4 May 2026).
  • Scale this delivery two ways: an in-house on-site team, plus established system integrators (Wipro, Infosys, TCS, PwC-class). The precedent: about 30,000 PwC professionals trained on Claude, Anthropic with Infosys, and OpenAI with TCS and Infosys (Anthropic↔PwC; Anthropic↔Infosys; OpenAI↔TCS). Move the work mostly in-house over a 3–5–7-year horizon.
  • In India the rules open the first procurement, so the lead deal is a contract to win and deliver, not a market to build from nothing.

02 · Origins & growth — global

Why sovereign AI became a thing

From 2017 to 2022, no government funded an AI lab just to avoid relying on another country, so the curve stayed flat. Then three forces — demand, supply and policy — turned five years of zero state funding into tens of billions in three years (CSIS).

What can go wrong

Three ways the read above could be wrong if the trend bends.

  • It is a hardware sales motion — as the Demand row's Investment Monitor cite notes, much sovereign-AI money still rents US infrastructure, so a vendor pulling back could cool the wave. Watch: compute deals that slip or shrink at renewal.
  • Announced, not deployed — the curve tracks money announced, not money spent, so the funding wave may be press releases more than working systems. Watch: programmes with no shipped citizen or enterprise product a year on.
  • The non-US base is Chinese — the realistic open base today is often Qwen, so a "neither US nor China" position is fragile until you train your own base. Watch: programmes still fine-tuning Qwen while claiming a third-way stance.
  • Every layer above silicon — models, data, applications, distribution — can be bought with money and patient government backing.
  • Silicon cannot be bought this way. It is rented, and US export policy sets the terms.
  • That gap limits how far money alone takes any programme.
  • So spending buys a real business, but the sovereignty still depends on US permission.
  • The Gulf spent the most and still runs on NVIDIA.
  • Europe's flagship is being bought out at the model-control layer that was meant to make it independent.
  • The programmes that get furthest on the least money succeed on what money cannot buy: their own language, their citizen data, and engineering efficiency.
  • Sakana, Typhoon, and the corporate-backed SEA players beat much larger state budgets that way.
  • For a country with little money, that points to owning the language, the data, and the citizen delivery while renting silicon openly.
  • That is the route India can realistically take. Section 03 — The India imperative is why it has no choice.

03 · The India imperative

Why India has no real choice but to run deployment sovereignty

  • Section 02 — Origins & growth leaves India one open path: own the language, the data and citizen delivery.
  • One Union Cabinet decision drove India's funding jump: it approved the ≈US$1.23B IndiaAI Mission on 7 March 2024 (Elets eGov) — but only ≈US$47.7M of it has been paid out in about two years (Medianama / Rajya Sabha reply).
  • India did not buy AI independence. It passed laws and paid subsidies to build it, the same way it once built hardware.

India is running its hardware playbook for software

  • India built electronics with a few state moves: pay for results not plans, pay down the hard-to-get input, back several Indian companies, require local build, and be the first big buyer through the government. The IndiaAI Mission applies the same moves to software.
  • Read each row left to right: the lever, its hardware form, its sovereign-AI form, and why the same logic carries over.

Pay for results, not for plans
  • Manufacturing PLI pays after a company delivers. It is not a grant paid up front.
  • It pays a 4%–6% cash reward on extra India-made sales above a base year, for five years (ELCINA scheme text).
  • It worked. ≈US$2.4B paid out brought ≈US$20.3B in investment and ≈US$190.9B in output by March 2025 (India Briefing).
  • The software version already exists. IndiaAI's Application Development Initiative pays in three stages: ≤US$6K (₹5 lakh) for a prototype, ≤US$30K (₹25 lakh) for a ministry pilot, then ≤US$119K (₹1 crore) plus a nationwide-rollout contract and four years of support (IndiaAI IADI FAQ).
  • A startup is paid only after its AI is piloted and used inside a government system (IndiaAI).
  • PLI paid for units shipped, not paper factories; IADI pays for AI running in government, not for a plan. Both release money only once the result exists.
Pay down the input that is hard to get
  • For hardware, the input that held builders back was the cost of setting up electronics and chip plants in India. PLI paid that cost down.
  • The Mission does not buy GPUs.
  • It signs up private clouds and pays down the cost of GPU time: 38,231 GPUs across 190 approved projects at ≈US$0.77/GPU-hour (≈₹65), as of 25 March 2026 (Newsgram).
  • That is roughly a third of the global cloud price (DQIndia).
  • Chip makers could not fund plant cost alone; AI builders cannot fund GPU time alone — the blocked input differs, the problem is the same. With the state paying for GPU time, a small Indian team can build and run AI here without a hyperscaler's budget.
Fund several Indian companies
  • PLI spread its rewards across a chosen group of Indian manufacturers. It did not pick one winner.
  • The Mission funds 12 organisations building foundation models.
  • The first builder selected under the Mission, on 26 April 2025, received a ≈US$11.7M compute subsidy on a ≈US$29.4M project, 4,096 H100 GPUs for six months via Yotta (TICE).
  • The 12-organisation group splits the money. The largest single grant is ≈US$126M in total support (₹1,058.52 cr) (BharatGen; Medianama / Lok Sabha reply).
  • The government spreads the money across a group of national builders rather than placing one bet, just as PLI spread rewards across manufacturers.
Require that it be made in India
  • Local-content rules made the subsidy build something India owned. They stopped it funding a foreign assembly line on Indian soil.
  • Funded companies build the full AI stack in India and keep it here — built, run and governed locally, with weights released openly. A funded builder runs as a full-stack, Indian-language-first platform serving enterprises, governments and developers, and gives the wider ecosystem low-cost models and tools to build on with full control (Funded builder — About).
  • India-trained models from a funded builder (30B / 105B, Apache 2.0, launched 6 March 2026, trained on open AI4Bharat Indian-language data) are governed in India and are a low-cost model the ecosystem builds on (Funded builder). IndicVoices alone covers 12,000 hours across 22 languages (AI4Bharat IndicVoices).
  • "Made in India" for software means the whole stack stays in the country: Indian-language-first, serving enterprises, governments and developers, and giving other Indian builders low-cost models and tools.
  • The India-trained model is one layer that builds a data advantage on regulated tasks; for other builders the same low-cost models and tools are the product they build on. No foreign vendor can take either away by changing a licence.
Be the first big buyer, as the government
  • Orders from the government and state-owned companies gave an Indian manufacturer a guaranteed buyer from day one.
  • The government is already buying.
  • The Ministry of Agriculture's Digital Crop Survey, delivered by a funded builder, ran for 400,000+ farmers across four states in October 2025 Company-reported (Sarvam).
  • An SBI Life deployment serves 80M+ customers in 11 languages Company-reported (Sarvam).
  • Tamil Nadu and Odisha signed state agreements (Sarvam).
  • Serving 1.4 billion citizens in 22 languages is a need no foreign lab meets on India's terms.
  • This gives Indian builders a guaranteed floor, not a blanket ban on foreign models. These sectors are mostly private and already use frontier models; the state-set demand is the opening where a regulator or a customer's own policy blocks a foreign model, or where the task is simple — exactly how government orders made the PLI manufacturer safe to back.
Help industry grow, don't just regulate it
  • India built its manufacturing strength with incentives and government orders, not with market limits.
  • India has a data law, not an AI law. The March 2024 pre-launch permission order was withdrawn within 15 days (AZB).
  • The India AI Governance Guidelines (5 Nov 2025) only say "do no harm". There is no standalone AI law (SCC Online).
  • The DPDP Rules were issued on 14 Nov 2025 and phase in by 14 May 2027 (Lexology / SAM).
  • India cannot out-build the others, and adding rules would not win it anything either.
  • So it keeps its options open by building rather than by restricting access — the opposite of the EU's access-control approach in Section 02 — Origins & growth.
Trajectory methodology

  • It tracks money promised, not money confirmed as spent.
  • Read it for the gap. The government paid out only ≈US$47.7M of the ≈US$1.23B budget in about two years.
  • That breaks down as ≈US$2.6M (₹21.79 cr, FY2024-25) plus ≈US$45.1M (₹379.15 cr, FY2025-26): ≈US$47.7M (₹400.94 cr) total, about 3.9% of the five-year budget (Rajya Sabha reply, 9 Feb 2026) (Medianama / Rajya Sabha reply; Down To Earth).

  • 2023 — US$41.7M — private money only: a sovereign-LLM builder (founded ≈Aug 2023) raises a US$41M seed plus Series A on 6 Dec 2023, India's largest AI round at the time, with no government money yet (TechCrunch).

Supply
  • The best chips sit under one country's export rules, as Section 02 — Origins & growth found, and no amount of Indian spending converts rented chips into owned ones.
  • India tried its own chips and hit a wall. Krutrim, India's first GenAI unicorn, stopped chip and model work and moved to cloud services on 5 May 2026, cutting 200+ jobs (TechCrunch).
  • If the best-funded Indian player quit its own silicon, no smaller team can win that fight.

  • Spend the small subsidy on training rather than on chip plants — the only supply approach a cash-short country can sustain, and the path Section 02 — Origins & growth pointed to.
Capital
  • The Gulf put in tens of billions from sovereign wealth funds and still runs on NVIDIA; a budget-tight state cannot win a spending war against that.
  • India's whole sovereign-AI budget rests on one ≈US$1.23B approval, and only ≈US$47.7M was paid out in about two years — so even the headline number is not cash in hand (Medianama / Rajya Sabha reply).
  • The government spreads its bets instead of trying to outspend anyone: the money is split across the 12-organisation group, so no single builder is funded at top-tier scale (Medianama / Lok Sabha reply).
  • With money this small and this slow, the only edge left is the one money cannot buy: India's own language, its citizen data, and lean engineering. It cannot outspend the others, so it competes on deployment instead.
Demand
  • A steady, lasting source of demand is what makes a cash-short challenger viable — the test Section 02 — Origins & growth set for India.
  • Serving 1.4 billion citizens in 22 languages, most with little English, is now a funded government mission, not a research goal, and no US or Chinese lab serves that on India's terms (Communications Today).
  • That demand is running work today: the Digital Crop Survey for 400,000+ farmers, Oct 2025 (Sarvam — DCS) Company-reported.
  • An SBI Life deployment has 80M+ customers in 11 languages Company-reported, plus Tamil Nadu and Odisha have signed state agreements (Sarvam).
  • 190 projects are already approved on subsidised compute (Newsgram).
  • India is the one big economy whose government demand is its own captive market — a language base, a citizen base and regulated sectors that only India can serve. Holding that market is what owning deployment means, and it does not turn on outspending anyone.
Policy
  • Big powers treat AI as a national asset: the US sells the whole stack, the EU controls access, and a country that wants to stay independent cannot fully accept either offer.
  • The US "AI Action Plan" (23 Jul 2025) promises to sell "secure, full-stack AI export packages – including hardware, models, software, applications, and standards" to allies (White House) — and taking that package means taking on the dependence that comes with it.
  • India chose to build first and regulate lightly, withdrawing the March 2024 pre-launch permission order within 15 days (AZB).
  • There are do-no-harm guidelines and no AI law. The DPDP rules are the only binding ones (SCC Online).
  • India hosted the first Global-South AI Impact Summit (16–21 Feb 2026). The declaration was backed by 92 countries and organisations (India AI Impact Summit).
  • Neither the US package nor the EU rulebook leaves India room to stay independent. Owning the deployment layer — language, data, citizen delivery — is the last credible "neither US nor China" position left, and the one India is set up to take.

What can go wrong

The path is sound on paper; three things can keep it on paper.

  • Announced is not spent — only ≈US$47.7M of the ≈US$1.23B has been released in about two years, so the funding curve measures political commitment, not money in builders' hands (Medianama / Rajya Sabha reply). Watch: the disbursed share against the five-year outlay each fiscal year.
  • The PLI analogy may not transfer — PLI paid on units shipped and verifiable incremental sales; sovereign-AI outcomes are services, far harder to meter, so the same pay-for-results gate may not work as cleanly for software. Watch: whether IADI defines a measurable, audited "in-production" trigger rather than a soft one.
  • The incentive only pays if the result runs in government — IADI releases the large stage-three money only after an AI is piloted and used inside a government system, so slow or stalled state adoption starves the scheme regardless of how good the models are. Watch: the count of stage-three deployment contracts actually signed, not pilots started.
  • A cash-short, demand-rich India has one path it can win, and it is already on it: own the language, the data and citizen delivery now. India's funding curve runs the opposite shape of the global one — a near-vertical line driven by a single Cabinet approval, with the money announced still far ahead of the ≈US$47.7M actually paid out. That curve is the plan working as intended: it applies the hardware PLI method to software, where a builder is paid only once its AI is in use inside a government system.
  • The thin, slow budget leaves no other option. India cannot out-build the Gulf's sovereign-wealth campuses, it cannot own the chips that almost no one outside China owns, and it cannot take the US full-stack package or the EU rulebook without losing its independent position. Deployment sovereignty is therefore the only path the numbers leave open. The open question now is who runs this path, and how well.

Fintech & BFSI
  • The push here comes straight from the RBI. Its FREE-AI Committee Report of 13 Aug 2025 sets out 26 recommendations across 6 pillars.
  • Recommendation 4 calls for a home-grown, finance-specific AI model and a way to fund it, to "mitigate excessive global LLM reliance" (Boston Institute of Analytics — verbatim Rec.4).
  • The date and structure are confirmed by Dvara — FREE-AI.
  • RBI's 2018 rule says all payment-system data must be stored only in India. A foreign model on that data must therefore run in an India region, store the data in India, or work through a system integrator. The rule limits how the model is used; it does not ban it (RBI Notification, 6 Apr 2018).
  • The 26 recommendations require a board-approved AI policy, an AI inventory, audits, red-teaming and incident reporting. That weight tilts the choice toward auditable models that keep the protected data on Indian soil (KPMG digest of RBI FREE-AI).
  • Indian BFSI already runs frontier models today, in an India region or co-deployed: Bajaj Finance (India's largest NBFC) on Azure OpenAI (Microsoft India — Bajaj Finance); ICICI Lombard's claims co-pilot on Azure OpenAI (Microsoft — ICICI Lombard); Razorpay's Agent Studio on Anthropic's Claude (Fintechnews — Razorpay / Claude); Pine Labs running OpenAI in its payments infrastructure (TechCrunch — Pine Labs / OpenAI).

When a rule or the bank's own policy rules out a foreign model, or the task is routine, the India-resident model carries it; when a frontier model is wanted and allowed, the bank uses that. One vendor accountable for both halves is what the buyer is looking for.

What can go wrong

The wedge holds only if a few load-bearing assumptions hold; here is where each could break.

  • The RBI driver is a recommendation, not a mandate — the FREE-AI Committee Report (13 Aug 2025) cited in the Fintech & BFSI row is the supervisor's own committee advising an indigenous finance model, not a binding order; only the 2018 payments-data localisation rule is enforceable today. If RBI never converts Recommendation 4 into a direction, the Fintech floor stays voluntary. Watch: whether RBI issues a master direction or circular that cites Recommendation 4.
  • The DPDP localisation hook is dated, not live — the DPDP Rules cited in the Government and Health rows were notified 14 Nov 2025, but the substantive provisions only come into force 14 May 2027, so the on-soil obligation many use-cases lean on is not yet enforceable and the timeline or carve-outs can still move. Watch: the staggered 14 Nov 2026 and 14 May 2027 enforcement dates holding without dilution.
  • The floor is a wedge, not the whole market — these sectors are mostly privately operated (≈92% of telecom subscribers, the largest port operator, ≈40% of bank assets) and the regulated buyers already run frontier models in production, so the rule only bites on protected data and regulated use-cases. The opening is an entry point an accountable provider extends from, not a captive market. Watch: whether the on-soil engagement broadens into the discretionary frontier workloads, or stays boxed into the compliance slice.
  • The law manufactures the demand, but the supply is thin. Most sectors have one Indian company working in them, and Energy & Utilities has none.
  • The companies that are there each run a narrow single-task tool or an AI built only for their own use. None runs a broad, country-hosted system across a whole sector.
  • The foreign and Asian models are good enough, and they already reach regulated Indian buyers through an India region or a system integrator — Indian BFSI runs Azure OpenAI, Claude and OpenAI in production today.
  • So the seven gaps collapse into a single role: one accountable provider that supplies the country-hosted multilingual model wherever the DPDP Act, critical-infrastructure status, an offline rule, the RBI's own recommendation or a customer policy demands it, and a frontier model wherever one is wanted and allowed.
  • The widest openings sit in Government & Public DPI and Fintech & BFSI, where the regulator is actively pushing, with Health and Defence close behind.
  • The next sections cover how India can build this, and which companies can.

06 · The go-to-market

Selling and running AI inside India is the part India can win. The rules guarantee a minimum amount of demand. The go-to-market lets one win repeat.

The sovereign-sector rules are a way in and a guaranteed minimum of demand, not the whole market. Most of these sectors are privately run, and Indian banks already use top global AI models today. What India can win is running the AI — building it, operating it, keeping it accountable on Indian soil, in Indian languages, for businesses, governments and developers. Call it the run-it-in-India role. It has two parts. First, be the one company the customer holds responsible for its AI: use the best global model when that is allowed, and an India-run model when a rule, a policy or the task requires it. Second, supply the wider set of Indian builders with low-cost models and developer tools, so developers, researchers and startups can build freely.

India can win the run-it-in-India role, not the model itself

Chips, GPUs and the most advanced model belong to better-funded US and Chinese labs, and no national AI programme makes its own chips — Saudi Arabia’s HUMAIN still buys NVIDIA chips (NVIDIA, 13 May 2025). India wins the run-it-in-India role instead: the channels, the 22 languages and the regulated workflows by which 1.4 billion citizens get AI, for businesses, governments and developers. Many Indian builders take part, building, deploying and governing the AI entirely in India.

The role to aim for: be the one company the customer holds responsible for its AI, not just a vendor that sells because a rule says so

The rules are a way in and a guaranteed minimum of demand, not the whole market. The State does not have a monopoly on the sovereign sectors, and Indian banks already use top global AI models today. So the win is being the one company responsible for the customer’s AI: use the best global model where allowed, and an India-run model where a rule, a policy or the task requires it. The rule already turns the hardest customer into guaranteed demand: the first builder chosen to build the sovereign LLM (26 Apr 2025) received the Mission’s largest subsidy — ≈US$11.7M of a ≈US$29.4M project, 4,096 H100 for six months — one of several funded builders, not a sole winner (TICE, 16 Jul 2025; Business Today, 16 Mar 2026).

The four steps that let one win repeat

STEP 01

Win a government customer first

  • Win the hardest, most secret customer — defence, the regulator, IndiaAI; once won, switching away is almost impossible.
  • In India the rule makes this customer required, not sold — the restriction on foreign models turns the hardest customer into demand the rule creates, rather than a sale won on price.
  • The defence, health or power-grid customer is still a contract you have to win.
→
STEP 02

Put your own engineers on-site to do the integration

  • Place engineers on-site to do the offline-system integration the customer cannot do — moving it from built to running in production.
  • In India: a team inside NHA/ABDM, a supervised bank, BSNL, a power distribution company or a defence Design Bureau, doing the on-site work no IT services firm will fund.
→
STEP 03

Build a reusable product, not a one-off project

  • Each setup hardens a reusable product; the next customer buys a nearly-standard configuration, not a custom build.
  • In India that product is an Indian-language model plus voice plus serving in a private cloud or on offline systems, spreading the unavoidable integration cost across the next bank, power company or state.
→
STEP 04

The first customer pulls in the rest of the market

  • The government-grade track record and hardened product pull in regulated commercial customers — Palantir’s commercial half grows faster after the first government customer, not before.
  • Where a rule or a company’s own policy blocks a foreign API, the DPDP-compliant sovereign system is the default in-house choice; foreign models otherwise come in only when the customer specifically chooses one and runs it on Indian servers.

Why each win makes the next one easier

Winning one sovereign customer makes the next easier, and so on. Reach is not the prize. It is what you spend to build something harder to copy: running the AI on Indian servers. Each stage has to happen before the one after it, and the last stage starts the next round.

STAGE 01

Reach

  • State channels and live customers put a sovereign model in front of regulated users at a scale no foreign vendor reaches.
→
STAGE 02

On-site engineers do the setup

  • The in-house team puts the model into the offline, India-based workflow the customer cannot set up alone.
  • The cost the rule makes unavoidable is itself a barrier to anyone who arrives later.

STAGE 03

Regulated data and feedback

  • Working inside the workflow produces multi-language, sector-specific data that by law must stay in India, is bound by the user’s consent and cannot be exported — something an arms-length API vendor does not build up here.
→
STAGE 04

A tuned model a shut-out rival cannot match

  • That owned feedback tunes the India-run model on exactly the tasks the sovereign customer is judged on; a rival shut out of the data cannot close the gap with computing power alone.
→
STAGE 05

Harder to switch away

  • The hardened, sector-standard product makes the next bank, power company or state buy a nearly-standard configuration — widening reach and starting the next round.

Additional considerations

Reach customers fast through large IT services firms, then shift the work mostly in-house

Your own on-site engineering team is the right long-run answer, but it cannot reach every regulated customer at once. The same labs that are building their own on-site-engineer ventures also deploy through large global IT services firms. So the fast path is to partner with a firm the size of Wipro, Infosys, TCS or PwC for immediate reach and for setup experience that improves your in-house team, then move the work mostly in-house over 3–5–7 years.

WHAT OTHERS ALREADY DO

Leading labs already reach customers through the big IT services firms

  • PwC and Anthropic are training and certifying 30,000 PwC professionals on Claude, inside Anthropic’s ≈US$100M Claude Partner Network that backs services firms which deploy AI (Anthropic, 14 May 2026).
  • Anthropic and Infosys built a centre focused on regulated industries, starting with telecom, with Claude built into Infosys Topaz (Anthropic, 17 Feb 2026).
  • OpenAI made TCS its way into the India market (Tata Group, 19 Feb 2026), and Infosys is one of seven global IT services firms — along with Accenture, Capgemini, Cognizant, TCS, PwC and CGI — signed up to roll out Codex (PR Newswire / Infosys, 22 Apr 2026; CRN Asia, 2026).
→
THE PATH OVER TIME

Use a partner’s reach now, do most of the work in-house later

  • Partner with an established IT services firm first, for immediate reach into the sector and for experience with committee-approved setups.
  • That experience improves the in-house engineering team, which takes over the standard-setting setups over 3–5–7 years — the reach is borrowed early and owned later.

The risk that someone copies the open model — and the protections that hold the advantage in place

The strongest objection: IndiaAI funding expects the model to be released openly, so a well-funded company that already sells to businesses in banking, health or logistics — but does not build AI itself — could take the open model, add AI coding tools to make up for its lack of AI staff, and ship a competing product without paying for the expensive build. The protection is a licence the builder chooses to limit this, plus the advantages, beyond any licence, that the regulated work itself creates.

THE RISK

A company already in the sector could copy the open model

  • A company already inside banking, health or logistics could take the open model and add Claude Code- or Codex-level AI coding tools to make up for its lack of in-house AI staff.
  • It then ships a competing product without paying for the model build — the case where the original builder is left out of pocket.
→
PROTECTION 01

A licence that limits this is available

  • IndiaAI does not require a fully-open, anyone-can-use-it licence — its CEO “didn’t want to dictate business models” and the builder keeps the intellectual property (MIT Tech Review, 4 Jul 2025; Outlook Business, 11 Feb 2025).
  • So a Llama- or Mistral-style community licence is allowed: it requires crediting the builder, naming any derived model, and following acceptable-use terms (Meta — Llama 3.3 Licence, 6 Dec 2024), with a revenue or size threshold — like Mistral’s trigger above US$20M per month — so small users can use it free without harm while large copycats must enter a paid relationship (Mistral AI, 9 Feb 2026).
→
PROTECTION 02

The lasting advantage is the role, not the licence

  • The lasting advantage shifts to the data-and-feedback loop and the product as a whole, not the model file itself (Interconnects, 13 Mar 2024); Mistral makes money from its open model by hosting it, plus private and on-site versions and fine-tuning subscriptions (Sacra, Jan 2026).
  • The original builder can credibly promise to cover a regulated customer’s legal costs over the model where a copycat cannot — IBM’s standard legal cover on its open Granite models is the example (IBM, 28 Sep 2023).
  • In short: even if the weights are open, what stays with the original builder is being hard to switch away from, official distribution, and legal cover for the customer.

What can go wrong

The go-to-market depends on a slow anchor customer and a borrowed channel; the main risks sit in timing, who owns the customer, and how concentrated early revenue is.

  • Government demand is real but lumpy and slow to pay — the rules guarantee the demand, but procurement timing is not in the builder’s control; an output-linked instrument pays only once the sovereign AI is live, so cash can lag a signed mandate by years. Watch: the gap between contracts won and revenue actually disbursed.
  • The IT-services partner could own the customer — the Wipro/Infosys/TCS/PwC-type firm used for fast reach also sits between the builder and the regulated buyer, so it can renew the relationship in its own name and disintermediate the builder. Watch: who signs the renewal and who the customer calls when the system breaks.
  • Early revenue concentrates on a few government anchors — the playbook wins the hardest customer first, which means the first years lean on a small number of defence, regulator or IndiaAI accounts; losing one is a large revenue hole before the commercial half scales. Watch: share of revenue from the top two or three accounts.
  • The shift from a partner’s reach to in-house delivery may slip — the 3–5–7-year transition to a mostly-in-house engineering team can stall if hiring and clearances lag, leaving the builder dependent on the channel longer than planned; this is separate from the open-weight-appropriation risk handled in the block above. Watch: share of standard-setting deployments still run by the partner versus the in-house team.
  • The part India can win is the run-it-in-India role: a top-grade open model is now a commodity, but reaching 1.4 billion citizens, the 22-language need and the rule to run on Indian servers are India’s own — a foreign vendor has no access to the channels and is blocked by the on-soil rule.
  • That role has a second part: supplying low-cost models and developer tools that put leading AI in the hands of the wider community — here the models and tools are the product the rest of India builds on.
  • Importing chips does not break this: computing power is rented and swapped, but the run-it-in-India role, the regulated-data feedback and the control layer cannot be — sovereignty lives in who controls the system, not who owns the chip factory.
  • The rules are a way in and a guaranteed minimum of demand, not the whole market: most of these sectors are privately run and Indian banks already use top global models, so the win is being the one company responsible for the customer’s AI — best model when allowed, own model when a rule, a policy or the task requires it.
  • The go-to-market has four parts: a PLI-style payment that comes through once the sovereign AI is live (the IndiaAI contest-to-contract route already exists); a deploy-together model (approved-supplier list, ministry contest, state agreement, on the customer’s own servers — as a sovereign builder did on a government identity programme); a large IT services firm as a channel for fast reach, shifting to mostly in-house; and an in-house on-site engineering team — the part Anthropic and OpenAI just spent billions building in 2026.
  • For a full-stack Indian builder — one among several, not a sole winner — the guaranteed minimum of rule-bound demand and an India-run model both speed things up, while the result depends on the in-house team that does the setup and makes one customer the standard for the whole sector before the 12-organisation group competes for the same contracts.
